C: Data Exploration and Summary Statistics¶

  • Tanush Parhi (Conclusion #1):

I studied the effect of different genres of video games on the total sales of video games. I performed several statistical tests to validate my hypothesis. Because the total sales data was highly skewed towards the right, the previous tests like ANOVA were not suitable. I started with the Levene's test to confirm the presence of homogeneity of variances and it was confirmed (W = 11.57, p ≈ 6.11×10⁻³⁶). I then ran the Kruskal-Wallis test and I found a significant relationship between the genre of a game and its total sales (H = 1498.52, p ≈ 9.21×10⁻³⁰⁷). S Since the Kruskal-Wallis test indicates only that there is some difference between different genres, I conducted further Dunn’s post hoc analysis (using Bonferroni adjustment to eliminate type I error) to see which genres are different from each other. The Dunn’s test revealed that 71 of the total 171 genre combinations had statistically significant difference in sales, especially niche genres such as visual novel and adventure, which didn’t perform as well as mainstream genres like sports, action, and shooter genres (p < 1×10⁻⁹⁰). The boxplot was used to demonstrate and confirm the same finding. Hence, the hypothesis that genre has no influence on sales has been rejected and genre was proved as one of the three most important predictors in the Random Forest model used later in the analysis.

  • Jake Glendon (Conclusion #2):

We begin by exploring the dataset's characteristics using descriptive statistics, which reveal a strong right-skew in total game sales across all platforms. Most titles generate relatively low sales, whereas a select few blockbuster games drive disproportionately high values. A prime example of this extreme volatility is Wii Sports, which stands out as a massive outlier with approximately 82.53 million units sold. This extreme skewness and high variability suggest that sales are unevenly distributed and heavily influenced by a handful of hit titles, though a noticeable relationship remains between categorical features like platform choice and overall sales performance. To evaluate whether platform choice significantly impacts sales, we utilized statistical hypothesis testing, though the data's inherent skewness requires careful interpretation. A Levene’s test confirmed that variance is not equal across platforms (W = 12.24, p = 1.0810^(-75), violating standard assumptions. Despite this, an ANOVA test was conducted to compare mean sales, yielding highly significant results (F = 25.23, p = 1.33 10^(-132)). Because the p-value is well below 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis, confirming that platform does have a statistically significant effect on sales.

  • Sang Pham (Conclusion #3):

To investigate whether release year has an effect on total global sales, Spearman rank correlation, linear regression, and Mann Whitney U were conducted. Because the total sales are heavily right skewed, with a small number of blockbuster titles inflating the mean, Spearman rank correlation was chosen as it is more robust to skewed distribution compared to something like Pearson. The Spearman test returned a correlation of p=-0.20 with p<0.001, which is well below the 0.05 significance threshold, so the null hypothesis was rejected and it was concluded that there is a significant negative monotonic relationship between release year and total sales. Linear regression on log transformed sales further confirmed this, returning a slope of -0.025 log units per year (p<0.001), meaning each additional year of release is associated with roughly a 5.6% decrease in sales per title on average. Finally, a Mann Whitney U test was used to directly compare the two eras without any normality assumptions, showing that early era games from 1995-2006 with a median of 0.19M statistically outsold late era games from 2007 to 2018 with a median of 0.12M (p<0.001). The violin plot confirms the same pattern visually. The early era violins are wider and sit higher with more mass above the 0.1M mark, while the late era violins compress and shift downward. The most likely explanation is market saturation, as the number of games released per year grew from around 216 in 1995 to over 1,500 by 2008 and 2009, which splits consumer spendings across far more titles at once. This finding also has implications for the rest of the analysis, as release year should be treated as a control variable since older platforms and genres may appear to perform better simply because they faced less competition at the time rather than anything related to the genre or platform itself.

2) Introduction¶

The video game industry has drastically evolved over the decades, from the earliest home platforms like the NES and Game Boy in the 1980s to the modern landscape of the mid-2020s dominated by high-performance PCs and consoles such as the PS5 and Xbox Series X. Over this time, the commercial success of a video game is often measured by its total sales worldwide which is often determined by a number of different factors, including genre, platform, publisher, release year, region sales, and critic score.

Behind every game, from the worst to the best, we wanted to determine whether any one of these factors has a meaningful impact on a game's total sales. Specifically, we focus on three key variables and their impact on the total sales of a video game across genre, platform, and release year. Using this knowledge, we wanted to build a model to predict the level of future success of video games, whether in development or at the brainstorming stage, based on their total sales and these factors.

Answering these questions matters beyond pure curiosity. In the background, there's a lot that goes into making video games, from getting investors to fund the project, planning the game's outline, building and developing the game, testing it, and then marketing it to the general public, which takes a lot of time and money. So, when investing in these games, you need to properly evaluate and plan and developers operating in an increasingly competitive industry, understanding which factors drive total sales can inform smarter decisions, such as which platform to build the game on, what type of game it should be, where to release it, and especially the budget.

As of late in the gaming industry, there have been cases where the budgets for games are over-inflated to the point where even if the game sells well, they aren’t able to recoup the cost, leading to layoffs and potentially full closures of the studios who develop said games. Some specific examples include games like Battlefield 6, which had a very successful launch back in October 2025, beating out that year’s Call of Duty, Black Ops 7, in terms of total concurrent player count. Yet despite this success, there were numerous layoffs at EA across the studios involved in developing the game. And a smaller example would be former studio Douze Dixième, who had released their game Mio: Memories in Orbit earlier this year. While critically successful based on the reviews, the studio did shut down this year as a result of the game not selling enough. Looking at the PC games platform Steam, 21,397 games released in 2025 on the platform alone, not accounting for all the older games as well, so making sure that keeping budgets in check is important based on the factors that we used later on.

By identifying the specific patterns that are common in commercially successful games and analyzing and comparing other successful hit games, companies can better align their products with consumer preferences and set realistic benchmarks, not as a guarantee of success, but as a way to better understand what separates a bust, a mid, or a hit video game.them while also understanding that the outcome of any release of any game can either underperform and become a bust, land somewhere in the middle, or become a major hit. 3) Data Curation¶

For our data, we chose datasets from Kaggle related to video game sales and their markets. For the first dataset by Kirubi, it covers and analyzes video game sales from 1980 to 2016, which includes columns such as name, platform, year of release, genre, publisher, North America sales, Europe sales, Japan sales, other sales, global sales, critic score, critic count, user_score, user_count, developer, and rating (Kirubi, 2016). The second dataset we found by Bohdan covers a broader and higher range of video games going up to 2024, analyzing each game’s market, which includes columns like image of game, title, console, genre, publisher, developer, critic_score, total_sales, north america sales, japan sales, europe sales, other sales, release date, and last update (Bohdan, 2025) . We decided to use both datasets because they would be useful for analyzing data, help include a broader range of video games, provide more analysis over the four decades of the video game industry, and fill any missing values or gaps in either dataset, ​ When preprocessing or transforming data, we import the datasets and convert them to dataframes. Then we parse and organize each dataframe, ensuring we extract useful columns and identify features or characteristics that are present and match across the dataframes, such as game name and platform. Next, we ensure that each dataframe has the same style and values by converting all string values to lowercase, rewriting column titles to match, extracting only the years from datetime objects as integers, dropping and filtering unneeded data or duplicates, and filling any empty values. Before merging, we first check whether the string values in both dataframes are in a similar format, so it's easier to drop duplicates of the same game. Once the merge was complete, we also added a new column with five categories that determined each game's sales level from poor, low, medium, high, and hit. We sorted each game's total sales into one of these categories so a game with total sales of 0.15 falls into the low category. We do this to fill in missing critic scores by grouping games by both genre and sales level, then using the median critic score for each group to replace the missing values.
